Justice for Joseph Lagerman

A new advocacy campaign, #JoeysLaw, has launched to spotlight the case of Joseph Lagerman, who is currently serving a 32.5-year sentence in Wisconsin for charges stemming from an incident in 2018. Supporters, led by Christy, aim to bring public attention to what they argue are serious judicial oversights and sentencing disparities.

"Joseph's story is more than a legal case — it's about truth, fairness, and human dignity," said Christy. "Through #JoeysLaw, we want to ensure his voice is heard, his story is told, and that justice is pursued."

The Facts

  • Zero injuries: No one was harmed.
  • No intent to harm: Ballistics prove a downward trajectory.
  • Mental health crisis: Diagnosed panic disorder at the time of the incident.
  • No criminal history: Joseph had no prior record.
  • Sentencing disparity: Similar cases average 5–7 years.

Why This Matters

Joseph's case is a chilling example of how the justice system can ignore context, mental health, and proportionality. His sentence doesn't just punish — it erases nuance, compassion, and truth.

Read Joseph’s Story

The ‘plea’ I took was really just me being pressured into something I didn’t understand. My lawyer told me it was my only option, but I didn’t know they could stack charges and enhancements the way they did. I thought I was agreeing to one thing, but I got sentenced as if I had confessed to everything in the book. I never had the chance to tell my side, not fully, not truthfully, not the way it should have been told in court.

That day in 2018, I was in a full-blown panic attack. I wasn’t in my right mind. I had been using, I was spiraling, and everything was closing in on me. When the gun went off, it wasn’t aimed at anyone. The ballistics show it. It was down, into the ground, not at a person. There were no injuries. No intent to harm. But the courtroom painted me like I had hunted people down. That’s not what happened.

I panicked and didn’t care what happened to me at that point. That’s the truth. I was in such a state of fear and chaos in my head that I couldn’t think straight. If you’ve never had your own mind turn into a trap, you can’t know what it’s like. They never let the jury or the judge hear about my mental health. They never let the truth breathe.

Witnesses who could have spoken on my behalf never even showed up. The charges got combined with the only other case I had, and suddenly it was like my entire life, every mistake, every shadow, got shoved into one trial. They sentenced me like I was some career criminal with a rap sheet longer than I am tall. But before that, I didn’t even have a record.

Thirty-two and a half years. For something where no one was hurt. For something where I never intended to hurt anyone. For something that, in similar cases, averages 5 to 7 years. That’s what justice looks like in Wisconsin, apparently.

I sit here now, years later, still stunned by how it all went down. I think about that courtroom — how quick it was, how much wasn’t said, how much was buried. I think about how my own words got twisted, how my so-called plea wasn’t a plea at all but a trap I walked into blind. I think about how my lawyer smiled at the judge while I stood there, my life being handed away in decades.

And I wonder: how many others are there like me? How many stories got buried because the system doesn’t have time for truth? Because deals are easier than trials, because stacking charges makes for a bigger sentence and a better headline? They bury us in silence and call it justice.

But I won’t stay buried. My voice, my truth, it’s still here. I may be behind these walls, but my words can still reach beyond them. That’s what #JoeysLaw is about. Digging up what they tried to bury. Shining a light where they wanted shadows. Saying, loudly, that justice without truth isn’t justice at all.

I’m Joseph Lagerman. I’m still here. Still fighting. Scars and all.
